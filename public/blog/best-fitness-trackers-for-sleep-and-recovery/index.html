What actually matters in a sleep and recovery tracker

The wearables market is saturated with devices that will tell you how many hours you slept. Fewer tell you anything useful about quality. If you're making training decisions based on recovery data, these are the metrics that matter:

  • HRV (Heart Rate Variability) — the most reliable single marker of autonomic recovery. Higher HRV generally indicates better readiness. The key is your personal baseline trend, not a single reading.
  • Sleep staging — time in light, deep (slow-wave), and REM sleep. Slow-wave sleep is where the majority of physical repair happens; REM supports cognitive recovery.
  • Resting heart rate overnight — elevated overnight HR often signals accumulated fatigue, illness, or under-recovery.
  • Respiratory rate — a useful secondary signal, often the first metric to shift when you're getting ill or overtrained.
  • A daily readiness or recovery score — a synthesised signal that combines the above into an actionable number, so you don't have to manually interpret five metrics every morning.

With that framework in mind, here's how the five main options compare in 2026.

Best overall for athletes

WHOOP Band

WHOOP is built from the ground up for recovery-focused athletes. It is a screenless band that measures continuously — HRV, resting heart rate, respiratory rate, skin temperature, and blood oxygen — then distils everything into a daily Recovery Score on a 0–100% scale. The score factors in sleep performance (actual sleep vs. your calculated need), HRV vs. your personal baseline, and recent training strain.

What makes WHOOP stand out for strength athletes specifically is the Strain Coach, which pairs recovery data with training load. If your Recovery Score is low, it will recommend lighter effort; if you're well-recovered, it will push you toward a harder session. This kind of feedback loop is genuinely useful when you're trying to decide whether to push or back off on a heavy squat day.

WHOOP also integrates with its own Strength Trainer feature for logging workouts — though if you want to see your actual lift progression over time (bench press trends, plateau detection, estimated 1RM), you'll need a separate tool. WHOOP Strength Trainer doesn't show exercise-by-exercise progression — that's a different layer.

Best for comfort & passive wear

Oura Ring

Oura Ring takes a different physical form factor — a titanium ring worn on the finger, which provides exceptional sensor contact and makes it genuinely easy to sleep in. For people who find wrist bands uncomfortable overnight or at the gym, this is a meaningful practical advantage.

The ring measures HRV, heart rate, skin temperature variation, SpO2, respiratory rate, and sleep stages. Its Readiness Score synthesises these into a daily signal that's comparable in quality to WHOOP's Recovery Score. Oura's sleep stage detection and temperature tracking are well-regarded in the quantified self community.

Where Oura differs from WHOOP is in its training context. Oura is excellent at recovery passively — it's less tightly integrated with workout logging or session strain. For athletes who also want deep training load analysis, WHOOP or Garmin will feel more complete. But if your primary goal is best-in-class sleep data in the most comfortable possible form factor, Oura is a serious contender.

Best simple smartwatch option

Fitbit Sense 2

Fitbit Sense 2 is the most accessible option on this list for people who want sleep and recovery tracking without committing to the complexity or cost of WHOOP or Garmin. It's a conventional smartwatch with a solid health sensor suite: sleep stages, SpO2, heart rate, skin temperature, and a passive EDA (electrodermal activity) sensor for stress tracking.

The Daily Readiness Score — available with Fitbit Premium — combines sleep, recent activity, and heart rate variability data into a simple readiness signal. It's less granular than WHOOP's Recovery Score or Oura's Readiness, but it's a reasonable proxy for people newer to recovery-based training decisions.

Fitbit Sense 2 makes sense if you want a single device that handles notifications, basic fitness tracking, sleep, and a readiness score — without the cost of Apple Watch or the subscription model of WHOOP. For dedicated strength athletes who want deep progression data, it's a starting point rather than an endpoint.

Best for deep training + recovery data

Garmin fēnix

Garmin fēnix series watches are built for athletes who want the most comprehensive training and recovery picture available in a single device. The recovery stack includes HRV Status (a trend-based HRV view over time), Body Battery (an energy reserves indicator), Training Readiness, sleep staging, and Pulse Ox — all available without a subscription.

Where Garmin pulls ahead of WHOOP for some athletes is in training analytics depth: VO2 max tracking, Training Load, Training Effect (aerobic and anaerobic), and GPS for outdoor sports. If you run, cycle, or do any cardio alongside strength training, Garmin gives you a unified picture that WHOOP doesn't match for that use case.

Best for Apple ecosystem users

Apple Watch

Apple Watch is the obvious choice if you're already in the Apple ecosystem and want a capable all-rounder. Recent models include sleep staging, overnight HRV measurement via the Health app, blood oxygen, skin temperature sensing, and heart rate tracking throughout the day.

Apple Watch doesn't produce a single daily recovery score the way WHOOP or Oura does — its health data is more granular and less synthesised into training readiness. For athletes who want a device that integrates deeply with iPhone, handles notifications, has a wide app ecosystem, and also provides health metrics, Apple Watch delivers. For athletes whose primary goal is recovery optimisation and training load management, dedicated trackers will generally go further.

Which one should you buy?

The right tracker depends on what you're optimising for:

  • Recovery and training readiness, above all else: WHOOP. Its Recovery Score and Strain Coach are purpose-built for athletes making daily training decisions.
  • Best sleep data, most comfortable wear: Oura Ring. If you find wrist devices uncomfortable overnight or want ring form factor, Oura produces comparable sleep quality data.
  • Deep training analytics + recovery in one device: Garmin fēnix. Best for multi-sport athletes who want GPS, training load, and recovery in one watch.
  • Apple ecosystem all-rounder: Apple Watch. Best if deep Apple integration matters and you want health tracking alongside everything else a smartwatch does.
  • Simpler entry point: Fitbit Sense 2. If you're new to recovery tracking and want a familiar form factor with a readiness score, this is the lowest-friction starting point.

Why the wearable is only half the story

A WHOOP Recovery Score of 85% is useful context before a session. But it doesn't tell you whether your bench press has improved over the last 8 weeks, whether your squat volume is trending up, or whether you've been stuck at the same weight for 4 sessions in a row. FAQ

Which fitness tracker is best for sleep tracking?

It depends on your priorities. WHOOP is widely regarded as the most actionable recovery tracker for athletes, combining sleep staging with HRV and a daily Recovery Score. Oura Ring is a strong alternative if you prefer a ring form factor and very comfortable overnight wear. Both go meaningfully deeper than most smartwatches.

Does WHOOP track sleep well?

WHOOP tracks sleep stages (light, REM, slow-wave), sleep performance vs. your calculated need, HRV, respiratory rate, and overnight heart rate. Its daily Recovery Score combines these into a single readiness signal — particularly useful for athletes making training decisions.

Is Oura Ring better than WHOOP for recovery?

Oura and WHOOP both produce high-quality sleep and HRV data. Oura is often preferred for comfort — a ring is easier to sleep in than a wrist band. WHOOP tends to provide more training-specific context and integrates better with workout logging for strength athletes.

Can Garmin track recovery and HRV?

Yes. Garmin fēnix watches include HRV Status, Body Battery, sleep tracking, and Training Readiness — all contributing to recovery context. Garmin's training analytics depth is a key strength, especially for athletes who also want GPS and performance tracking.

What's the best fitness tracker if you strength train?

For recovery context, WHOOP and Garmin fēnix are the strongest options. For exercise-by-exercise lift progression — bench press trends, plateau detection, estimated 1RM — no wearable currently fills that gap. StrengthInsight is built specifically for that layer, using screenshots from WHOOP Strength Trainer or Hevy.

Does Fitbit Sense 2 track sleep and recovery?

Fitbit Sense 2 includes sleep stages, Daily Readiness Score (with Premium), SpO2, skin temperature, and stress tracking via EDA sensor. It's a capable option for people who want sleep and wellness tracking in a familiar smartwatch form factor.
